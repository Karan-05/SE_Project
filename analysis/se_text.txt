Agentic Decomposition for Reliable Long-Horizon
AI Planning in Competitive Environments
Karan Allagh Raz Saremi
Department of Computer Science
New York University
New York, NY , USA
{ka3527, rsaremi}@nyu.edu
Abstract—Large Language Models (LLMs) excel at many
tasks, but struggle with long-horizon, multi-step problems due
to compounding errors [6]. We propose anagentic decomposition
framework that breaks complex tasks into focused subtasks
distributed across multiple specialist agents, drawing on pre-
dictive modeling, reinforcement learning (RL), and chain-of-
LLM prompting. Our approach integrates: (1) Multi-modal
outcome prediction, to foresee which tasks are likely to fail
or succeed, enabling proactive adjustments; (2) Adaptive RL-
based task selection, to dynamically allocate effort to high-value
opportunities; and (3) LLM decomposition chains, where a team
of LLM agents collaboratively solve subtasks with local error
correction and voting. Experiments on22k crowdsourced coding
challenges demonstrate that incorporating structured features
and graph embeddings yields near-perfect outcome classification
(F1 up to 0.99). An RL agent trained in a competitive simulation
achieves a95%win rate (vs.81%for a baseline) while reducing
uncompleted (“starved”) tasks by∼5×. For long-form problem
solving, agentic LLM workflows outperform single-call baselines,
solving tasks with zero errors where unstructured prompts con-
sistently fail beyond a few steps [6]. A Pareto analysis shows our
selective decomposition strategy approaches the success of full
decomposition while using 40% fewer tokens. We discuss how this
paradigm—inspired by human teamwork and crowdsourcing—
enhances reliability, transparency, and adaptability in complex
AI systems [1], [5], [10].
I. INTRODUCTION
Complex real-world problems often require numerous rea-
soning or decision steps in sequence. Even small errors can
snowball over long sequences, causing standard monolithic
AI agents to break down [6]. Recent studies underline this
fragility: advanced reasoning models succeed on short tasks
but “illusion of thinking” experiments (e.g., extended Towers
of Hanoi puzzles) found that performance collapses beyond
∼8 steps [6]. This poses a fundamental obstacle for deploying
AI in domains like competitive programming, project man-
agement, or multi-stage workflows, where sustained accuracy
over many dependent steps is required.
Agentic decompositionhas emerged as a promising solution.
Instead of relying on a single agent to perform a complex
task end-to-end, the task is divided among many lightweight
agents or subtasks, each responsible for a small part [5]. By
structuring intelligence as a team effort, this approach contains
errors locally and allows targeted fixes or verifications at each
step [1]. Prior works have drawn analogies to crowdsourcing:much as human workflows decompose big projects into man-
ageable pieces, AI chains decompose reasoning into modular
subcalls [5], [10]. Notable examples include MAKER (Maxi-
mal Agentic Decomposition withk-threshold voting) which
reported a million-step LLM execution with zero mistakes
by orchestrating many micro-agents with voting-based error
correction [2]. Likewise, Wu et al. demonstrated that chaining
LLM prompts (AI Chains) can improve transparency and
controllability in human–AI interaction [1].
In this paper, we advance the state of the art in agentic
AI systems along three dimensions. First, we incorporate
predictive modelingto anticipate task outcomes and inform the
agentic strategy. Our case study is a large dataset of 22,023
crowdsourced coding challenges (from Topcoder), where each
challenge either succeeds (has a winner) or fails for various
reasons (no submissions, no valid winner, etc.). We train super-
vised classifiers on multimodal features—including textual de-
scriptions, temporal milestones, and a graph-based embedding
of the participant–task network—to predict these outcomes.
By injecting structural context (e.g., a learned Node2Vec rep-
resentation of the worker–task graph), we achieve substantial
gains in predictive F1 scores (e.g., +6.8% for identifying failed
challenges). These predictions allow an AI agent tofocus or
decomposetasks that are flagged as high-risk, thereby applying
agentic resources where they are most needed.
Second, we develop areinforcement learning agentthat uses
the above predictions and environment feedback to optimally
choose which tasks to tackle and how. We frame the challenge
management process as an episodic Markov Decision Process:
at each time step the agent can select a pending challenge
to address (or a no-op), earning rewards for completed chal-
lenges. Using a competition simulator, we compare a random
policy, a heuristic skill-matching policy, a contextual bandit,
and a Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent. The DQN consistently
achieves the highest average reward per episode and win rate
(95.1% win ratio), significantly outperforming non-learning
baselines. Notably, the learned policy reduces the fraction of
“starved” tasks (challenges that receive no valid submissions)
from 24% under random selection to just 5%. This demon-
strates the potential of RL to dynamically allocate effort in a
multi-task setting, complementing the static predictions.
Third, we introduce anLLM multi-agent workflowto exe-
cute the steps of complex tasks. Our framework assigns spe-

cialized LLM agents to different subtasks, enabling a division
of labor [3]. For example, in a coding challenge scenario,
one agent may analyze requirements, another writes code, and
another reviews or tests the output. Inspired by role-based
verification pipelines [3], we implement a “Planner” agent to
decompose and assign subtasks, “Solver” agents to propose
solutions, and a “Verifier” agent to validate results and flag
errors. The agents communicate via intermediate prompts, and
we incorporate a simple voting mechanism wherein multiple
solver agents attempt the same subtask in parallel and the
verifier selects a consensus answer [2]. By breaking down
problems and introducing redundancy, the multi-agent chain
is more robust to individual errors: if one agent fails, others
can catch and correct it, preventing errors from propagating.
A key challenge in such a system is the trade-off between
solution quality and cost. Fine-grained decomposition with
many agents can approach perfect accuracy, but at the expense
of additional token usage. We therefore implement anadaptive
meta-controllerthat decides, for each new task, whether to
invoke full agentic decomposition or a lighter strategy. This
decision is based on predicted difficulty (from the supervised
model) and a small lookahead search: a learned policy estimat-
ing whether the marginal gain in success probability outweighs
the extra token cost [8]. In evaluations, this meta-selector
achieved a near-Pareto-optimal balance, often choosing direct
routes for easy tasks and decomposition for hard tasks. The
result is that our adaptive approach attains almost the same
success rate as always decomposing, while saving roughly
40% of the tokens on average.
We evaluate our integrated framework on both real-world
challenge data and controlled long-horizon reasoning tasks. On
the real challenge dataset, our system can forecast problematic
challenges and autonomously navigate them: for instance, it
can preemptively decompose a “very likely to fail” challenge
into smaller subtasks to solicit focused solutions, or choose
to skip/defer a low-value challenge predicted to be unsalvage-
able. On the controlled side, we construct a benchmark of
algorithmic puzzles (including an expanded 20-disk Tower
of Hanoi, which demands220−1≈106correct moves).
Single-agent baselines fail on the hardest puzzles (consistent
with prior reports [6]), whereas an agentic LLM pipeline can
solve longer horizons by employing many micro-agents and
consensus voting per step [2]. This extreme case corroborates
theoretical scaling advantages of agentic decomposition [2].
In summary, our contributions are:
•We demonstrate how incorporating structured features
(metadata, graph embeddings) into outcome prediction
for complex tasks can drastically improve foresight,
achieving up to 0.99 F1 in classifying challenge outcomes
and enabling early identification of likely failures.
•We develop a multi-agent RL approach for task selection
in a competitive environment, which significantly boosts
overall success rates (95% win rate vs. 81%) and reduces
the incidence of neglected tasks by a factor of five.
•We propose an agentic LLM workflow that decomposes
tasks among specialized agents with local error correc-tion. Our approach solves tasks of high complexity with
very low error, and we introduce a meta-decomposition
policy to optimize the success/cost trade-off.
•We provide comprehensive evaluations, including abla-
tion studies for feature impacts and strategy comparisons,
and identify design principles for future agentic AI sys-
tems, such as balancing human-in-the-loop guidance and
automated coordination.
II. RELATEDWORK
LLM Chain-of-Thought and Decomposition:There is
growing interest in techniques that enable LLMs to tackle
complex queries via intermediate steps. Chain-of-Thought
prompting can improve performance on math and logic prob-
lems, but still uses a single-agent paradigm. More advanced
are chained LLM workflows and Automatic Chain-of-Thought
(auto-CoT) approaches that break a problem into multiple
prompt calls [7]. Early work by [1] introduced AI Chains to
let users compose transparent multi-step LLM processes, and
subsequent studies extended this to self-consistency (sampling
multiple reasoning paths and selecting the majority). V oting
and consistency checks can markedly increase reliability over
naive single-shot answers [2]. Our agentic decomposition
builds on these ideas by using multiple agents in parallel
for different sub-tasks and employing a structured, role-based
aggregation.
Hierarchical and Multi-Agent AI:Our approach relates to
hierarchical reinforcement learning and multi-agent systems.
In hierarchical RL, a high-level controller delegates to low-
level workers—analogous to our meta-controller orchestrating
specialist LLM agents. Multi-agent LLM systems attempted to
build autonomous “societies” of agents, often without formal
guarantees. We ground our multi-agent design in role spe-
cialization and verification pipelines [3]. Uluslu et al. (2025)
on robust NLI through agentic decomposition used agent
pipelines (syntax/lexical experts, etc.) to improve robustness
under adversarial hints [3]. Their results support dividing tasks
by competency and aggregating insights, aligning with our
findings in coding challenges.
Tool Use and Planner–Executor Architectures:Another
line of research integrates tools or structured planning with
LLM agents. Planner–executor loops interleave reasoning and
actions (e.g., running tests, querying databases). Dynamic task
graph generation work highlights evaluation beyond accuracy,
including structural quality of decomposition [4]. Our success-
vs-cost analysis (Figure 1) complements this by treating de-
composition depth as a tunable efficiency parameter.
Multi-Modal and Graph Representations:Predicting
challenge outcomes benefits from combining text, temporal
signals, and relational context. Node2Vec embeddings over
participant–challenge graphs capture solver communities and
challenge similarity. The observed F1 gains from graph fea-
tures (+0.024to+0.068absolute) underline the importance
of structural context in these prediction tasks.

III. METHODOLOGY
A. Problem Setup and Data
Our primary experimental setting is online coding com-
petitions. We collected data from Topcoder, encompassing
over 22k challenges held over several years. Each challenge
includes a textual description, metadata (prize money, regis-
trants, etc.), timestamps (registration open/close, submission
deadline), and final outcomes (submissions, winner status,
cancellation reasons). We focus on predicting and optimizing
three outcomes:
1)Has Winner:successful challenge (at least one passing
submission awarded first place).
2)Failed (No Winner):no winner awarded (could be no
submissions or all submissions fail review).
3)Starved/Dropped:cancelled early due to insufficient
participation (e.g., zero registrants or zero submissions).
We also predict two continuous outcomes: number of submis-
sions, and winning score.
B. Supervised Outcome Prediction
We trained supervised models for the above outcomes.
Feature sets included:
•Text features:TF-IDF and embedding-based features
from challenge descriptions (we also used CodeBERT
vectors).
•Time features:registration/submission durations, time-
of-day, and deadline indicators.
•Metadata:prize amount, required tech stack, newcomer
flags, etc.
•Graph embeddings:Node2Vec embeddings over a bi-
partite participant–challenge graph; we used challenge
embeddings and aggregate statistics of registrant embed-
dings.
We evaluated logistic regression (elastic net), Random Forest,
and Gradient Boosting classifiers (plus regressors for numeric
targets). Models were trained on an 80/20 train-test split (time-
based split to reduce leakage). Table I summarizes classifica-
tion performance.
In addition, regression models for number of submis-
sions achieved low normalized RMSE (we omit details due
to space). Overall, the supervised module provides high-
confidence signals that can feed into the agentic decision
process (e.g., challenges predicted with>95%chance of

C. Reinforcement Learning Agent for Task Selection
We model challenge management as an episodic RL prob-
lem. Each episode simulates a sequence ofNchallenges and
the agent selects one challenge to focus on (or no-op) at
each time step. The state includes features of currently open
challenges. Selecting a challenge yields reward if the challenge
ends successfully; we set reward proportional to prize value.
We include a small time penalty to encourage efficiency.
We compare:Random(uniform choice),Skill-Match(rule-
based choice based on a fixed expertise profile),Contextual
Bandit(one-step expected-reward model), andDQN(two-
layer network; replay; discounting). Table II reports aggregate
metrics over 100 test episodes.
D. LLM Agentic Decomposition Workflow
The agentic workflow solves an individual complex task by
dynamically constructing a directed acyclic graph of subtasks.
APlannerdecomposes and assigns subtasks;kSolveragents
propose solutions per subtask (we usedk= 3); aVerifier
checks consistency/correctness and selects a consensus, op-
tionally triggering rephrasing or further splitting. This mirrors
a project-manager pattern integrating specialist outputs.
E. Adaptive Decomposition Strategy
Full multi-agent decomposition is token-expensive, while
many tasks are solvable with a single call. We implement a
meta-strategy to decide between (i) single-agent solve / light
CoT, and (ii) full agentic workflow. The decision is based on
predicted difficulty and (optionally) a budget threshold [8]. In
a mixed benchmark, always-decompose achieves 96% success
at high token cost, single-agent achieves 72% at low cost,
and the adaptive strategy achieves 90% with moderate cost.
Figure 1 illustrates the trade-off.

We evaluate the full system end-to-end.

ulation on historical data, the predictor flags high-risk chal-
lenges; the RL agent decides whether to invest effort; and
difficult cases trigger LLM decomposition. Under reasonable
assumptions (e.g., LLM-generated guidance increases solver
participation probability), we estimate reductions in failure
rates on flagged challenges.

on long-horizon puzzles (e.g., Tower of Hanoi at increasing

V. CONCLUSION
We presented an agentic decomposition framework inte-
grating prediction, decision-making, and multi-agent LLM
problem solving to tackle complex tasks with high relia-
bility. Experiments show that structured features and graph
embeddings enable strong outcome foresight, RL allocation
improves global success, and multi-agent decomposition with
local verification enables longer-horizon problem solving than
monolithic baselines. By “smashing the problem into atomic
steps” and coordinating specialized agents, we can approach
levels of reliability that single models struggle to sustain [2],
[6]. Future work includes live deployment, adding human-in-
the-loop verification, and learning decomposition policies end-
to-end [4], [8].
